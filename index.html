Section I
The Problem: Cambridge Builds It, Others Scale It
Cambridge's early-stage ecosystem is world-class. The problem begins at Series B. A structural funding gap at the growth stage means that companies which the University has nurtured — often over years or decades — are forced to seek capital in the US, sell to foreign acquirers, or list abroad. The economic and strategic value leaves Cambridge even when the company nominally stays.

The Core Problem in One Sentence. Cambridge creates the IP, trains the founders, and provides the early capital — but at the growth stage, when companies need £50m–£200m to scale internationally, the UK domestic capital market cannot provide it, and so the strategic and economic value of a decade of university investment is captured abroad.

A. The Scale-Up Gap — Where the Funding Falls Short

The UK has a strong early-stage ecosystem. Seed and Series A funding for Cambridge spinouts is well served by Parkwalk, Cambridge Enterprise Ventures, Cambridge Innovation Capital, Martlet, and a dense network of angel investors and EIS funds. The problem begins at Series B and worsens at Series C and beyond.

At the late stage — rounds of $100m and above — UK domestic investors provide just 9% of funding. The US provides the majority. This is not a new problem, but it is getting worse: the UK domestic share of late-stage deep-tech funding hit its lowest recorded level in 2025, even as the total amount of investment into UK deep-tech reached record highs. More capital is flowing in, but almost none of it is British.

It shows up in some of Cambridge's most celebrated companies — and in each case, the absence of domestic or MENA growth capital meant that strategic control, listing venue, or operational centre of gravity shifted abroad. These are not failures. They are missed opportunities for Cambridge to retain what it created.

The Pattern

In every case, the same story: World-class technology developed at or near Cambridge. Early-stage funding from UK sources. Growth-stage capital from Japan, the US, or other foreign investors. Strategic control, listing venue, and long-term economic value flowing abroad. GCC sovereign capital — patient, large-scale, strategically aligned — is conspicuously absent in every case, despite having stronger commercial rationale than most of the investors who did participate.

B. Six Benefits GCC Capital Brings That No Other Investor Does

01
Patient Capital at Scale
GCC SWFs collectively manage over $6 trillion. They can write £50–£200m cheques into a single company and hold for 15+ years — precisely what deep-tech companies need and what UK institutional capital cannot provide. The scale-up gap is fundamentally a patience and scale problem. Sovereign capital solves both.
02
MENA Market Access
A GCC sovereign investor brings more than capital — it brings a direct route into Gulf government procurement, sovereign partner introductions, and anchor customer relationships across healthcare (M42), energy (ADNOC, Aramco), and technology (G42, HUMAIN). This accelerates commercial validation and international revenue by 18–24 months.
03
Reduced Exit Pressure
Traditional VC fund cycles create forced exits — companies are sold before reaching full value because a fund needs to return capital to LPs. Sovereign capital has no such constraint. Cambridge companies backed by GCC sovereign funds can grow to their full potential before an exit is needed, retaining more value in the UK ecosystem.
04
Strategic Alignment with Vision 2030
Saudi Vision 2030 and Abu Dhabi's diversification agenda target the exact sectors Cambridge excels in: semiconductors, AI, biotech, clean energy, advanced manufacturing. GCC sovereign investors are not seeking passive returns — they are building national technological capability, and Cambridge spinouts are precisely the vehicles to do it through.
05
Geopolitical Diversification
Dependence on US capital for UK deep-tech growth creates geopolitical risk. US investors bring US strategic priorities, US listing preferences, and US exit preferences. GCC capital — politically aligned with the UK, non-CFIUS constrained, UK-relationship-positive — diversifies the strategic influence on Cambridge's growth-stage companies.
06
A New Exit Route: MENA IPOs
The Tadawul (Saudi stock exchange) and Abu Dhabi Securities Exchange are fast-maturing capital markets hungry for deep-tech listings. A Cambridge company with GCC sovereign backing, MENA revenues, and Gulf government relationships has access to a third major exit route — alongside Nasdaq and LSE — increasing optionality and competitive tension in exit processes.

Section IV
The Opportunity: What Cambridge Enterprise Is Uniquely Positioned to Do
Cambridge Enterprise sits at the intersection of two worlds: the Cambridge spinout ecosystem and the global investor community. It is the only institution that can systematically bridge the two — and in doing so, make GCC sovereign capital a durable structural feature of the Cambridge growth-stage ecosystem rather than an occasional occurrence.

A. The Strategic Position Cambridge Enterprise Holds

Cambridge Enterprise has a unique institutional position that no VC fund, bank, or advisory firm can replicate. It has trusted relationships with founders across every Cambridge department. It has early visibility on which spinouts have the technology, team, and market timing to reach the growth stage. It has the credibility of the University of Cambridge behind every introduction it makes. And it has existing investor relationships through its own fund activity and co-investment network.

This position means Cambridge Enterprise can do something that individual investors cannot: systematically identify the Cambridge companies most suited to GCC capital, prepare them for sovereign investor engagement, and provide the trusted institutional introduction that turns a cold approach into a warm conversation.

The Trusted Intermediary Role. GCC sovereign investors do not need a directory of Cambridge companies — they can find those themselves. What they lack is a trusted institutional partner who can curate, validate, and introduce the right opportunities at the right stage, with the right preparation. Cambridge Enterprise is the only institution that can credibly play that role for the entire Cambridge ecosystem.

C. What This Asks of Cambridge Enterprise

This is not a large resource ask. The programme does not require a new fund, a new team, or significant capital. It requires Cambridge Enterprise to play an institutional coordination role it is already partially playing — but systematically, and with GCC sovereign investors as an explicit target constituency. The primary inputs are: relationship investment (meetings with GCC investors), curated deal flow (already exists through the portfolio), and founder preparation (helping Cambridge founders understand GCC capital as an option). The return — retaining more of the value Cambridge creates, within the ecosystem — is substantial.
